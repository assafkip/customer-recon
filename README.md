# customer-recon

A threat-intelligence approach to knowing your market. Built with Claude Code.

I spent 12 years doing threat intelligence. The work was reconnaissance. Build a picture of a target from what they say in the open, before you ever make contact.

Knowing your customer is the same work.

Most founders research the market by asking their friends and reading their own assumptions back to themselves. That is not recon. That is a mirror.

Your customers are already talking. On Reddit, in forums, in reviews, in the threads where they complain about the tools they pay for. They use specific words. They have specific pains. They name the thing they hate. Most of it is public.

So stop guessing. Run recon.

This is the system I use. Six prompts. About 25 minutes per market. Drop them into Claude, fill the blanks, get a brief.

## What it produces

- **Watering holes.** Where your buyers actually gather. The subreddits and communities they live in, not the ones you assume.
- **Voice of customer.** What they love and hate, in their own words. Pulled from real threads, quoted, linked.
- **Pain map.** The recurring pains, ranked by how often they come up and how much they sting.
- **Competitor read.** What people actually say about the alternatives. Where every option falls short.
- **The objection.** The reason your buyer will not switch, even when they hate what they have.
- **The wedge.** The gap nobody fills, and the exact language to describe it. Their words, not yours.

## Quickstart

1. Open Claude Code (works best with web access on, so it can read live threads).
2. Open [`SYSTEM.md`](SYSTEM.md).
3. Run the six prompts in order. Answer the questions about your market. Paste in threads when asked.
4. You get a brief. See [`example-brief.md`](example-brief.md) for what it looks like filled in.

No setup. No API key. No account. Copy, paste, run.

## Why this works

Threat intel taught me that people tell you the truth when they think they are talking to each other, not to you. A complaint thread is more honest than any survey. A review nobody was paid to write is worth more than a focus group.

The founders who win read the open source. The ones who guess build for a customer who does not exist.
